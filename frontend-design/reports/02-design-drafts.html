FRONTEND DESIGN STRATEGY - PART 2 OF 3

Three Launch-Pragmatic Frontend Design Drafts

Three meaningfully different directions for CompeteIQ, grounded in the Part 1 research report and the current Next.js 15 + Tailwind + lucide-react app. The drafts preserve launch-critical stability while showing how the product can mature after revenue starts.

How to read this

The research report shows the same pattern across Crayon, Klue, Similarweb, Linear, and Perplexity: users trust real product output, clear citations, simple navigation, and answer-first reports. They reject noisy dashboards, tab sprawl, and enterprise friction. These drafts translate that into three product directions.

Draft 1 - Clear Signal (fastest launch) Draft 2 - Briefing Room (highest value) Draft 3 - Radar (post-launch brand bet) Recommendation matrix

All wireframes are low-fidelity HTML boxes. They describe structure, hierarchy, and page intent, not final production pixels.

DRAFT 1 - FASTEST PUBLIC LAUNCH

"Clear Signal"

A polish pass on what already exists. Light, trustworthy, direct, and shippable without changing the backend or adding a component system.

1 - Design nameClear Signal
2 - Positioning angleCompetitor intelligence without the enterprise mess. One clear report, one clear next move.
3 - Best-suited userSolo founders, small business owners, consultants, and operators who need a useful answer today.
4 - Landing pageTop nav, direct hero, real report preview, trust strip, 3-step flow, four proof-led feature cards, single Pro card, FAQ, footer.
5 - DashboardCurrent dashboard pattern with stronger hierarchy: plan badge, free-report remaining state, business form, generate CTA, recent reports.
6 - Report pageAnswer-first report: executive summary, top market gaps, recommended actions, competitor summaries, analysis sections, copy button.
7 - NavigationKeep the current horizontal dashboard nav. Dashboard, Reports, plan badge, account, sign-out. No sidebar before launch.
8 - Colour schemeExisting sky-blue brand on white and gray-50. Green for active, amber for trial/generation, red only for failures.
9 - TypographyExisting next/font Inter. Tighten hierarchy through size and weight, not new font dependencies.
10 - UI componentsReuse current .card, .btn-primary, .btn-secondary, .input, badges, and lucide-react icons.
11 - Button styleSolid brand primary, restrained secondary, icons only where they clarify action. One dominant CTA per screen.
12 - Card styleWhite cards, 1px border, soft shadow, rounded-xl, generous padding. No nested-card complexity.
13 - Form / inputCurrent single-column and two-column inputs; clearer helper text and inline error copy. Server URL validation remains authoritative.
14 - Table / reportStacked report sections instead of dense tables. Lists and numbered actions make the output fast to scan.
15 - Pricing pageOne Pro card: $79/mo, unlimited reports, up to 5 competitors, secure Stripe billing, one free report before paying.
16 - MobileSingle-column stacks, current nav remains simple, report sections stay readable, CTAs are full width where useful.
17 - Trust elementsReal report preview, no credit card, secure billing via Stripe, one free report, source list where available.
18 - Supports conversionReduces friction. The user sees the report value before signup and has one obvious action: generate the free report.
19 - Supports efficiencyDirect setup, no new navigation model, no configuration burden, no dashboard firehose.
20 - Inspired byPerplexity's low-friction first action, Similarweb's at-a-glance comparison, and the current app's launch constraints.
21 - Why it fits CompeteIQIt protects the Friday-launch goal. It improves trust and conversion without creating a new QA surface.

DRAFT 2 - HIGHEST PERCEIVED VALUE

"Briefing Room"

A premium but restrained SaaS direction. It makes the report feel like a decision-grade briefing instead of a generic AI document.

1 - Design nameBriefing Room
2 - Positioning angleYour AI competitive briefing: concise, cited, and built for business decisions.
3 - Best-suited userConsultants, agencies, growth leads, and founders who need the product to feel worth $79/mo.
4 - Landing pageHero with report preview, "anatomy of a report" showcase, use-case sections, trust strip, pricing, FAQ, and a sample-report CTA.
5 - DashboardStill simple, but more polished: KPI row, business card, recent report cards, plan status, and clearer report generation panel.
6 - Report pageReport is the product showpiece: executive summary first, opportunity cards, competitor comparison, section jump links, source/citation chips.
7 - NavigationKeep top nav at first. Sidebar can wait until the dashboard has enough density to justify it.
8 - Colour schemeNavy and sky become the premium brand base; amber is reserved for market gaps and opportunities.
9 - TypographyUse current Inter immediately. Post-launch, tune font variables or add a display weight only if it does not create layout churn.
10 - UI componentsPre-launch: existing components. Post-launch: selectively introduce shadcn/Radix for tabs, tooltips, dialogs, and table primitives.
11 - Button styleSolid sky primary, restrained navy outline secondary, small ghost text links for lower-priority actions.
12 - Card styleMore structured cards with header rows, section labels, and tighter spacing. Premium, not decorative.
13 - Form / inputBusiness and competitor form becomes more guided: examples, URL count, validation copy, and save/generate continuity.
14 - Table / reportComparison matrix only where it improves scan speed. Keep the primary report narrative readable.
15 - Pricing page$79/mo framed against DIY research time. Include "one free report", "secure billing", and "cancel anytime" copy.
16 - MobileReport jump links collapse into a compact section nav. Comparison areas become stacked cards.
17 - Trust elementsSample report, source chips, "pages scanned" count, Stripe billing, no card for first report, clear failure states.
18 - Supports conversionShows the value of the report before asking for money; makes the upgrade feel like buying recurring intelligence.
19 - Supports efficiencyThe report opens with what matters now, then lets users drill into evidence only when needed.
20 - Inspired byKlue's consumption surface, Similarweb's comparison clarity, Linear's restraint, Perplexity's trust through sources.
21 - Why it fits CompeteIQIt is the best long-term SaaS brand path because it feels serious without becoming enterprise-heavy.

DRAFT 3 - POST-LAUNCH DIFFERENTIATOR

"Radar"

A more memorable product identity: command input, radar metaphor, watchlist thinking, and opportunity/gap scoring. Strong brand potential, but not the safest pre-launch move.

1 - Design nameRadar
2 - Positioning angleYour always-on competitive radar for market gaps, threats, and moves worth tracking.
3 - Best-suited userBootstrapped founders and operators who want a sharp tool with a stronger personality.
4 - Landing pageCommand-style hero input, report-building preview, radar/watchlist motif, punchy pricing, and stronger product voice.
5 - DashboardCommand center with watchlist chips, report cards, gap/opportunity score, and recent scan activity.
6 - Report pageBriefing memo with verdict, gap score, watchlist action, cited evidence, and a compact competitor head-to-head.
7 - NavigationCompact rail or command bar later. Do not introduce before the current product has enough repeat-use density.
8 - Colour schemeNear-black UI, paper-toned report content, electric lime CTA, cyan data, coral alerts.
9 - TypographyLater: display grotesk plus mono metrics. Pre-launch: do not add this complexity.
10 - UI componentsScore meters, command input, watchlist chips, source counters, and motion-safe progress log.
11 - Button styleHigh-contrast lime primary, dark outlines, crisp focus glow, smaller radius.
12 - Card styleDark panel cards and paper report surfaces. Needs careful contrast testing.
13 - Form / inputGlobal paste-a-URL command input. Strong first-action UX, but it changes the feel of the whole product.
14 - Table / reportMemo sections plus compact mono comparison table and score callouts.
15 - Pricing pageBold single Pro card: "Stop guessing. $79/mo." Still keeps one free report and Stripe trust copy.
16 - MobileCommand bar and score meters must be simplified. Motion should reduce or disable on mobile.
17 - Trust elementsSources scanned count, citations, clear score explanation, Stripe billing, no-card first report.
18 - Supports conversionMemorable input-as-hero and score mechanic create a stronger hook than generic SaaS layouts.
19 - Supports efficiencyScore and watchlist give returning users a fast read without opening every report.
20 - Inspired byPerplexity's input-first UI and Linear's dark restraint, but made more ownable for competitive intelligence.
21 - Why it fits CompeteIQIt can become a stronger brand signature once the core funnel is proven and customers want repeat tracking.

Recommendation matrix

GoalBest draftWhy
Fastest public launchDraft 1 - Clear SignalUses the existing app structure, component classes, palette, and routes. Lowest QA and dependency risk.
Highest perceived valueDraft 2 - Briefing RoomThe report feels like a decision-grade briefing, which better supports the $79/mo offer.
Strongest long-term SaaS brandDraft 2 - Briefing RoomIt creates credible SaaS polish without forcing a risky identity change or heavy pre-launch tooling.
Most distinctive future brandDraft 3 - RadarGap score, watchlist, and command input are ownable, but they should wait until the core funnel is live.
Lowest-cost solo operationDraft 1 - Clear SignalFewest dependencies, simplest maintenance, easiest for Codex or Claude Code to modify safely.

Suggested path

Launch with Draft 1 - Clear Signal. Pull in Draft 2's best content ideas immediately where they cost almost nothing: real report preview, answer-first report hierarchy, stronger trust copy, and pricing value framing. Save Draft 2's component-system polish and Draft 3's radar/gap-score identity for post-launch work.
